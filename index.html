LANGUAGE VISUALIZATION

“Synesthetic Cryptograms” explore the hidden patterns of speech by translating sounds into visual maps.

In the spirit of cryptogram puzzles that I enjoyed throughout my childhood, this series takes units of speech sounds (phonemes) and shows them as blocks of colors based on the sounds’ features. The top image shows the place of articulation for a stream of speech: units of sound that are articulated at the front of the vocal tract are shown as blocks of light colors, sounds at the back as dark colors. The lower image shows airflow patterns for the same stream of speech: the release of air via sonorants is represented in blocks of cool colors, punctuated by obstructions to airflow in warm colors.

Source data is generated with the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary, an original Python script, and-- in this particular case-- the prologue to “Romeo and Juliet”.

NARCISSUS TAZETTA

In kindergarten, my class planted Narcissus tazetta plants, and recorded their growth through daily drawings. Twelve years later, I repeated the exercise. Like the early versions, these later drawings begin as scientific observations; they use drawing as a means of seeing and studying. But in changing the scale and placing the viewer within the roots, the later drawings do more than just record. They intentionally mystify these forms that I love, and open them up to new interpretations and new explorations.

ORIGAMI

The natural world is filled with repetition: a few simple manipulations, when repeated enough times, create something surprising and complex.

Origami has a similar aesthetic. I manipulate a single sheet of paper in one of two ways: with a "valley" fold or "mountain" fold. Done repeatedly, these folds interact with each other to create new shapes with volume, lines, flexibility, and movement.

MOVING LINES

In 2011, I drew five drawings every day, each taking no more than a minute. Working at that pace, I found myself drawing sequences: watching the lines on my pages move, disappear, reappear, turn into recognizable objects, and then back into marks.

"Moving Lines" builds on that exploration of lines as transient marks, sometimes obviously connected to an artist’s hand, and sometimes (seemingly) with an identity and direction of their own.

STUDIO ASSISTANTSHIP

Working as a studio assistant to Daniela Rivera, I painted assigned portions of Rivera's Accidental Memling Gul and built wooden infrastructure (like this 15 ft. painter’s bench) for the 360 sq. ft. painting in progress. The painting was shown at the 2010 Foster Prize Exhibition at Boston’s Institute of Contemporary Art.

SKETCHBOOK

Like a journal, my sketchbooks show where I’ve been, how I’ve observed, and the thoughts and ideas I’ve explored over time. Sometimes the sketches turn into fully realized projects, but more often than not, they stand for themselves as a trace of who I am.
