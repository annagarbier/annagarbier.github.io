LANGUAGE VISUALIZATION

All speech can be broken into building blocks of sound. Like atoms, these sounds have characteritics that make them distinguishable from each other. In the spirit of cryptogram puzzles that I enjoyed as a kid, "Synesthetic Cryptograms" take a stream of speech and encode the sounds of that speech into blocks of color.

The top image shows the place of articulation for the prologue to "Romeo and Juliet". Sounds articulated at the front of the vocal tract are represented with light colors, sounds at the back with dark colors. The lower image shows airflow patterns for the same stream of speech. The release of air via sonorants is represented in cool colors, pucntuated by obstructions to airflow in warm colors.

Source data is generated with the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary and an original Python script.

MOVING LINES

In 2011, I drew five drawings every day, each taking no more than a minute. Working at that pace, I found myself drawing sequences: watching the lines on my pages move, disappear, reappear, turn into recognizable objects then back into marks.

"Moving Lines" builds on that exploration of lines as transient marks, sometimes in my control, and sometimes (seemingly) with a direction of their own.

PAPERWHITES

In kindergarten, my class planted paperwhite bulbs, and recorded their growth through daily drawings. Twelve years later, I repeated the exercise. Though still observational in process, these later drawings aren't scientific in purpose. They're larger-than-human landscapes, maps of natural forms no longer recognizable.

ORIGAMI

The natural world is filled with repetition: a few simple manipulations-- when repeated enough times-- create something surprising and complex.

Origami has a similar aesthetic. With my hands, I manipulate a sheet of paper in one of two ways: with a "valley" fold or "mountain" fold. Done repeatedly, these folds interact with each other to create new shapes with volume, lines, flexibility, and movement.

PAINTER'S BENCH

Working as a studio assistant, I created this 15 ft. artist’s bench as one of several logistical solutions required to paint a 360 sq. ft. oil painting in a limited studio space. The painting by Daniela Rivera, titled Accidental Memling Gul, was part of the 2010 Foster Prize Exhibition at Boston’s Institute of Contemporary Art.
