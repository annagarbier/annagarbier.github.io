LANGUAGE VISUALIZATION

A typical cryptogram puzzle consists of a sequence of letters such that systematically replacing each of them for another letter reveals a well-known piece of text.

In the same spirit, “Synesthetic Cryptograms” takes the building blocks of speech and encodes those blocks in colors based on articulatory features. The top image shows the place of articulation for a well-known piece of speech. Units of sound that are articulated at the front of the vocal tract are shown as blocks of light colors, sounds at the back as dark colors. The lower image shows airflow patterns for the same stream of speech. The release of air via sonorants is represented in blocks of cool colors, punctuated by obstructions to airflow in warm colors.

Source data is generated with the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary, an original Python script, and-- in this particular case-- the prologue to “Romeo and Juliet”.

PAPERWHITES

In kindergarten, my class planted paperwhite bulbs, and recorded their growth through daily drawings. I learned to look actively, to use drawing as a way of observing and thinking.

Twelve years later, I repeated the exercise, this time focusing in on my favorite part of the plants: their roots. Though still observational in process, these later drawings aren't scientific in purpose. They're larger-than-human landscapes, maps of natural forms no longer recognizable.

ORIGAMI

The natural world is filled with repetition to surprising effect. Take DNA for example: a few manipulations-- when repeated enough times-- create complex living diversity.

Origami has a similar aesthetic. With my hands, I manipulate a sheet of paper in one of two ways: with a "valley" fold or "mountain" fold. Done repeatedly, these folds interact with each other to create new shapes with volume, lines, flexibility, and movement.

MOVING LINES

In 2011, I drew five drawings every day, each taking no more than a minute. Working at that pace, I found myself drawing sequences: watching the lines on my pages move, disappear, reappear, turn into recognizable objects then back into marks.

"Moving Lines" builds on that exploration of lines as transient marks, sometimes obviously connected to an artist’s hand, and sometimes (seemingly) with an identity and direction of their own.

PAINTER'S BENCH

Working as a studio assistant, I created this 15 ft. artist’s bench as one of several logistical solutions required to paint a 360 sq. ft. oil painting in a limited studio space. The painting by Daniela Rivera, titled Accidental Memling Gul, was part of the 2010 Foster Prize Exhibition at Boston’s Institute of Contemporary Art.
