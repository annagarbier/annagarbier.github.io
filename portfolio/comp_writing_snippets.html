Computational writing snippets

The following are notes for an ongoing computational writing project, updated periodically.

Over the past year, I created several mini text generation programs. Through these programs, I tested different generative methods including basic string operations, Markov models, context-free grammars, and neural networks. Because none of these programs can generate language out of nothing, I also tested various input texts: I fed my language-hungry programs everything from novels, to Craigslist ads, to restaurant menus, to simple lists of words.

These exercises resulted in little narrative "snapshots" made mostly of existing language around us. For example, a book review made of wine descriptions, a tale of recycled goods from Craigslist, an imaginary still-life from the Met collections:

Along the way, I've also wondered whether a particular programming language may guide me towards specific types of computational writing. I added different languages into the mix. For example, the same little program written in Perl, Python, and Clojure.

# Perl
my $file = path("craigslist.txt");
my $content = $file->slurp_utf8();
my @strings = $content =~ /because [^\.\!\,]*/g;

print("Free...\n");
foreach my $s (@strings) {
print(" ${s}\n");
}

# Pytnon
with open('craigslist.txt', 'r') as f:
print('Free...')
for match in re.findall(r'because[^\.\!\,]*', f.read()):
print(' ' + match)

; Clojure
(defn -main []
(println "Free...")
(doseq [match (re-seq #"because [^\.\!\,]*"
(slurp (io/resource "craigslist.txt")))]
(apply println (concat [" " match]))))

What next? I'll be creating a longer-form generative piece written in Clojure that builds new narratives from the everyday language of the Internet (e.g. restaurant menus, user manuals, reviews).
